LMIA Visa

Canada Offers an Effortless LMIA Visa Application Process for Employers

An LMIA, or Labour Market Impact Assessment, is a crucial process managed by Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC). It helps employers figure out if they genuinely need to hire foreign workers when they can't find any Canadians or permanent residents for a specific job. While some employers can hire foreign workers without an LMIA, most need it to help their employees get valid work permits.

ESDC, the government department in charge of many social programs and services in Canada, looks at several factors when deciding whether to approve an offer of employment to a foreign worker:

  • The offered salary or wage, adjusted based on the job's location.
  • Any current labor disputes involving the employer.
  • Making sure the job conditions meet labor laws.
  • Checking if there's a lack of workers in the job's area.
  • Reviewing the foreign worker's qualifications, considering their work experience and education.
  • Looking at whether hiring the foreign worker could create job opportunities for Canadians and permanent residents.

As for how long it takes to process an LMIA application in Canada, it usually takes about three months. However, ESDC has promised to process LMIA requests in specific employment categories, like high-demand jobs, skilled trades, jobs with top wages, and short-term jobs, within 10 days.LMIA job offer requirements for employers include:

  • Paying a processing fee of $1,000 and an additional 'Privilege Fee' of $100.
  • Demonstrating efforts to advertise the job vacancy on the Canadian Job Bank and at least two other hiring platforms.
  • Showing sincere attempts to hire Canadian citizens and permanent residents before considering foreign workers.
  • Committing not to terminate or reduce the working hours of Canadian citizens and permanent residents because of hiring a foreign worker.
  • Presenting job offers primarily in English or French. Listing additional languages as job requirements is generally discouraged unless justified.
  • Providing financial documents to prove the ability to support the foreign worker's salary for a specified duration.
